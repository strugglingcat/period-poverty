Luxury Goods?

In 2020, 30 states still charge a "luxury goods" tax on menstrual products.

The infamous "tampon tax" brings each state millions of dollars of yearly revenue, which is why many are so reluctant to repeal it.

Disproportionate impact on low-income women

A 2019 study by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists showed states tax menstrual products between 4.70% (Hawaii) and 9.90% (Louisiana). The mean tax was 7.41%.

The result? A heavier financial burden on marginalized low-income women and heavy bleeders and observable repercussions including decreased participation in education, work, and social activities.

1 out of 3 parents

are worried about their ability to afford menstrual products due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Twenty-two states considered repealing the luxury goods tax on menstrual products in 2019.

The New York Times detailed how only Rhode Island and California (temporarily) repealed it.

In total, only 20 out of 50 states have removed the tax thus far.

In the U.S.

41% of children live in low-income families, according to news reports and the U.S. Census

42 million impoverished women in the U.S.

2/3 low-income women interviewed "did not have the resources to buy menstrual hygiene products at some point during the last year."

1/5 respondents struggle to afford period products on a monthly basis.
